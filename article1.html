Brexit Creeping on Investors

by Scott Tindle | Mon January 9, 2017

What's going on here?

Britain’s Prime Minister, Theresa May, reiterated her government’s commitment to leaving the European Union (EU) over the weekend – and the market didn’t like what it heard! The pound fell more than 1% on Monday – to its lowest level in months versus both the dollar and the euro.

What does this mean?

There’s a big debate in Britain concerning what sort of deal the UK government should seek in its expected upcoming negotiations with the EU regarding formally leaving it. Investors are generally squeamish over any signs that Britain will lose access to free trade with the EU – which European countries have said will happen if Britain does not accept the principle of open immigration with the EU. Investors seemed to interpret May’s comments as indicative of her government prioritizing leaving the EU (including its immigration pact) over preserving the existing trading relationship.

Why should I care?

For the markets: The value of the pound is perhaps the best indicator of investors’ views of the Brexit process. The value of the pound is, to a large degree, an indication of the market’s expectations of the volume of investment in Britain in the future: if more companies are going to invest in Britain, it makes sense that the pound will go up (because their investment would be in the local currency obviously). If post-Brexit Britain is less attractive to businesses, it’s more likely that the pound will suffer (as it did on Monday).

So far, Britain’s economy has weathered the six months following the Brexit vote very well, with various measures of economic activity hitting their highest levels in more than a year. However, nothing has actually happened yet: Britain is yet to formally begin the Brexit process. As this begins (which is expected by the end of March), the realities of the post-Brexit environment should, gradually, become clearer.
